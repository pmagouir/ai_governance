# Facilitator Protocol: The Quality Auditor

**Task:** pilot-task-3-critique | **Window:** Weeks 5-6 (May 4-15, 2026) | **Participants:** 10 across 6 units

---

## When to Review

**During the task window:**
- [ ] Day 3: Check submission tracker — who has started? Nudge anyone who hasn't opened the task.
- [ ] Day 7: Mid-window check — review any completed initial assessments. Flag anyone stuck at Below Standard for personal outreach.
- [ ] Day 10: Final check — anyone who hasn't submitted initial draft needs direct contact.

**After automated assessment runs:**
- [ ] Spot-check 2 participants (20% sample) — compare your assessment to the skill's automated assessment. Note any disagreements for calibration.
- [ ] Review all Below Standard flags — these participants may need facilitator encouragement alongside the automated reteaching module.
- [ ] Review all Exceeds Standard — confirm the extension challenge is appropriate and the participant isn't coasting.

---

## What to Prioritize

**Read first:** The self-audit of their own Task 2 work (Part B). Part A (external audit with planted errors) has a known answer key and the automated assessment handles it well. Part B is where the real signal lives — it is cognitively harder to find errors in your own work than in someone else's. The self-audit reveals whether the participant has developed genuine critical evaluation skills or is only good at following checklists.

**The one thing that matters most:** Can they find errors in their OWN work, not just the planted errors in someone else's?

**Common patterns to watch for:**
1. **Catches planted errors but declares own work "fine."** They find 4 out of 5 planted errors in Part A but then audit their own Task 2 work in Part B and report no meaningful issues. This is the most common pattern and the biggest red flag — it suggests critique as performance rather than genuine evaluation.
2. **Lists errors without severity reasoning.** They identify issues but treat all errors as equally serious. A missing citation and a governance violation get the same treatment. Watch for flat lists with no severity calibration.
3. **Improvements are generic rather than citing a specific finding.** The Prompt Improvement Memo (Part C) says things like "be more specific" or "add more context" instead of tracing the improvement back to a specific error found in Parts A or B. The corrective transfer loop is broken if the improvements don't connect to the findings.

**What NOT to spend time on:** Whether they found every planted error in Part A (the automated assessment handles this), the formatting of the audit log, or whether their Part C prompt improvements are "good enough" to fully prevent errors. Focus on the self-audit honesty and the finding-to-improvement connection.

## Caseload Reality

You have 10 participants and a full CSAO workload. Here's how to manage this in ~2 hours per task cycle:

| Activity | Time | When |
|----------|------|------|
| Submission tracker check | 10 min | Days 3, 7, 10 |
| Spot-check 2 participants | 30 min | After initial assessments |
| Below Standard outreach | 15 min/person | As flagged |
| Exceeds Standard review | 10 min/person | As flagged |
| Revision spot-check | 20 min | After revisions |
| **Total per cycle** | **~2 hours** | |

**Triage rule:** If you can only review 2 participants deeply, choose: (1) the Below Standard participant most likely to disengage, and (2) the Exceeds Standard participant who could become a peer teaching resource.

---

## Quick Reference

- **Automated assessment handles:** Initial scoring, feedback delivery, revision guidance, reassessment, portfolio packaging
- **You handle:** Encouragement, calibration spot-checks, Below Standard outreach, peer exchange facilitation, escalation decisions
- **Escalation trigger:** If a participant scores Below Standard on the same dimension across two consecutive tasks, schedule a 1:1 conversation.
