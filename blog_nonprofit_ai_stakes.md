# Ninety-Two Percent of Nonprofits Use AI. Seven Percent Report Major Impact.

*On governance, staff development, and the opportunity hiding in plain sight.*

---

That gap is the central challenge for our sector right now.

The 2026 Nonprofit AI Adoption Report, drawing on surveys of 346 organizations, found that 65% of nonprofits describe their AI use as reactive and individual — one-off prompts, personal experimentation, no shared systems. Only 4% have documented, repeatable workflows. Fewer than half have a formal governance policy. The tools are widely available and genuinely capable. The constraint is governance and design — the gap between individual tool use and organizational transformation.

---

## The Case for Urgency

DC CAP is a Washington, D.C.-based nonprofit. We combine multi-year scholarships with intensive success coaching and strong university partnerships to help low-income, first-generation DC students get through college with minimal debt. We run that at $13.7 million in operating expenses, serving 1,290 scholars across 13 university partnerships, with a staff that stays deliberately lean. Every dollar not spent on overhead goes toward scholarships and direct support. The math of our mission requires that we get more efficient every year, because demand grows faster than funding.

That is the structural condition of the social sector, not a DC CAP-specific problem. Federal funding uncertainty, tighter overhead scrutiny, and growing service demand are squeezing nonprofit operating models across the country. Research suggests organizations that integrate AI into repeatable workflows save 15 to 20 hours of staff time per week on administrative tasks. Organizations that capture those gains built governance and shared systems first. Individual experimentation, on its own, does not compound.

## What DC CAP Built

In late 2024, DC CAP launched the Innovation Hub and began developing the Claude Pilot Implementation Plan — a structured, enterprise-wide effort to bring AI into our operations in a way that would actually compound. KPMG selected DC CAP for a $600,000 AI Impact grant as part of their initiative to help education-focused nonprofits integrate AI with real depth. We are in the first weeks of a 60-day leadership pilot as I write this.

The first decision we made was to build a competency framework before touching a single workflow. Organizations that jump to tools before governance are the ones stuck at 65%.

We identified four competencies every staff member needs to work effectively alongside AI — the 4Ds. Description is the ability to communicate with an AI system precisely enough to get useful output. Delegation is knowing which tasks belong to AI and which require human judgment, including where AI breaks down. Discernment is evaluating outputs critically before acting on them, treating every result as a draft your expertise must complete. Diligence is the consistent application of accuracy, privacy, and ethical standards to every AI-assisted workflow.

These map to three operating modes: Automation for repeatable tasks, Augmentation for complex work where human expertise remains central, and Agency for sophisticated multi-step problem-solving.

We launched with a kickoff presentation for the full cohort and built the supporting infrastructure publicly at dccapinnovation.org — a full governance framework, activity guides built around real DC CAP scenarios, a data classification practice tool, a KPI framework, weekly check-in instruments, and a pre-launch fluency assessment that established baseline data before any content was delivered. Nineteen deliverables, built by two people using the same tools they were teaching.

Six functional units now have senior leaders building unit-specific AI workflows. By Week 8, each will have produced a deployable module grounded in their actual work. We call it "Your Unit. Your Module. Your Legacy." The deliverable belongs to the unit after the pilot ends.

The returns already show up in the data. Success coaches reduced administrative time by more than 50%, redirecting that capacity toward student relationships and early identification of stop-out risk. Two people built a 19-deliverable infrastructure in a few months. Governance and a clear framework were the enabling conditions.

## The Staff Development Argument

Organizations have a responsibility here that goes beyond operational efficiency.

The World Economic Forum estimates 60% of the global workforce will need meaningful retraining by 2027. For context, only half of those workers currently have access to adequate training through their employers. SHRM data shows 36% of employees planning to resign within a year cite inadequate development as a driver. LinkedIn data shows job postings requiring AI skills have grown 3.5 times faster than overall postings since 2021. Ninety-four percent of CEOs identify AI as their top in-demand skill; only 35% feel they have prepared their employees effectively.

Compensation competitiveness is already a structural challenge for nonprofits. Organizations that build real AI fluency programs will hold a retention and recruitment advantage over those that don't. A staff member who gains genuine AI competency at your organization carries that forward — they get better at their job, they contribute more to the team, they have a clearer argument for their own growth. That return is not available through salary alone.

The approach matters as much as the investment. Training that happens in the flow of actual work produces far better outcomes than isolated training events. Our leaders are redesigning workflows in units they already own, using scenarios drawn from their real challenges. The capstone is a deliverable they will use. That is the distinction most upskilling programs miss.

## What We Can Learn from Each Other

Organizations with formal governance structures capture dramatically more value from AI than those without. The sophistication gap tracks with resource capacity — meaning the organizations that most need efficiency gains are also the most likely to remain stuck in ad-hoc use.

That is fixable if organizations that have done this work share it. The DC CAP pilot site is public for exactly that reason. The governance framework, competency model, activity guides, and facilitation protocols are all accessible at dccapinnovation.org.

We are still learning. Our pilot ends in June, and the pre-launch baseline will tell us what actually moved. I expect to find things that did not work the way we anticipated, and we will publish those findings alongside what did. None of this is revolutionary. It reinforces the pattern visible in every organization that has built something durable: invest in the invisible infrastructure, measure what matters, and adjust from evidence.

If you have built an AI program generating real returns in a lean-staffed, mission-driven context, I would genuinely welcome hearing from you.
