Session Facilitation Guide

DC CAP Enterprise AI Leadership Pilot

Building leaders who build AI capability in their teams.

The Opening Ritual

Every Session Starts Here

The question:

"What's the worst prompt you wrote this week, and what did you learn from it?"

Instructions for Facilitator

  • Weeks 1–3: Preston goes first. Model that bad prompts are expected and instructive.
  • Keep it tight: 30 seconds per person. The point is normalization through repetition, not deep analysis.
  • If someone hasn't used Claude: Respond with "That's useful data too. What got in the way?" No judgment. Just curiosity.
  • By Week 4: Ask a champion to go first instead. This signals the ownership shift.

Why this matters: Psychological safety is built through repeated modeling. Every leader in the room learns that struggle is signal, and the facilitator is the first one to signal it.

Session Structure by Phase

Weeks 1–3: Preston-Led Foundation

Total time: ~35 minutes

  • 2 min: Opening ritual
  • 10 min: Activity walkthrough or demonstration
  • 15 min: Hands-on practice (everyone works in Claude simultaneously)
  • 5 min: Share one discovery or question
  • 3 min: Preview next week + remind about weekly check-in
~35 minutes total

Goal: Establish the ritual, build confidence, normalize learning by doing.

Weeks 4–6: Co-Facilitated Transition

Total time: ~35 minutes

  • 2 min: Opening ritual (champion goes first)
  • 5 min: "Show and Tell" — 2 participants present their week's experiment (2.5 min each)
  • 10 min: Group problem-solving on a challenge someone is facing
  • 10 min: New technique or advanced application (Preston or champion)
  • 5 min: Module-building check-in — "Where is your unit's training module?"
  • 3 min: Preview + check-in reminder
~35 minutes total

Goal: Shift ownership to champions. Move from "I learned" to "I taught."

Weeks 7–8: Champion-Led Sessions

Total time: ~35 minutes

  • 2 min: Opening ritual (champion leads)
  • 10 min: "Show and Tell" — 3 participants present experiments
  • 15 min: Group problem-solving or workflow redesign review
  • 5 min: Module peer review — one leader presents their unit module draft for group feedback
  • 3 min: Capstone preparation reminder
~35 minutes total

Goal: Champions own the session fully. Preston observes and advises only.

Recommended Weekly Activity Map

Which Activity, Which Week, Which Competency

Activities should follow a Delegation-first sequence. Leaders need to understand when to use Claude before they learn how to prompt effectively. Governance (Diligence) threads through every week rather than front-loading.

Week Activity Primary 4D Why This Week
Week 2 Delegation Matrix Delegation Before leaders learn to prompt well, they need to decide which tasks belong with Claude and which stay human. This prevents the "use AI for everything" trap.
Week 3 Prompt Lab + Governance Walk Description Diligence Now that leaders know what to delegate, teach them how to communicate with Claude effectively. Pair with the Governance Walk so data classification becomes immediate habit.
Week 4 Data Tier Practice Diligence Two weeks of active Claude use means leaders have real questions about what data is safe. The interactive classification exercise hits harder when they have context from their own work.
Week 5 Skill Trigger Challenge Delegation Leaders are ready to discover the skill library. This deepens Delegation fluency by showing them the full range of pre-built capabilities available for their unit's work.
Week 6 Output Audit (The Trap) Discernment Five weeks of Claude use means leaders trust it. The polished-looking email with three hidden errors teaches them to verify rather than accept. This is where Discernment becomes real.
Weeks 7-8 Module Peer Review + Capstone Prep All 4Ds Capstone integrates everything. Leaders present their unit's environment design for peer feedback, demonstrating all four competencies in practice.

The key principle: Delegation before Description, Diligence threaded throughout (not front-loaded), Discernment after trust has built, and integration at the end. Each activity lands hardest when leaders have enough real-world Claude experience to connect the exercise to their own work.

The Failure Wall

A Shared Space for Learning Together

The Failure Wall is a shared space (Monday.com board or Teams channel) where participants post failed prompts and what they learned. This is where the cohort learns at scale.

Ground Rules

  1. Preston and champions post first to establish the norm. Failure-posting is not optional; it's part of the culture.
  2. Celebrate the most instructive failure each week during the opening ritual. Name it, applaud it, explain what the cohort learned.
  3. Frame failures as data: "This prompt didn't work because..." teaches the whole cohort how to diagnose their own mistakes.
  4. Never critique a failure post. Only respond with "Here's what I'd try" or "I had a similar experience." This is not a place to judge; it's a place to build shared knowledge.

Facilitator tip: If the Failure Wall goes silent, it's a sign that psychological safety hasn't landed yet. Go back to modeling. Post your own failure, speak about it in the opening ritual, and invite a shy participant to share theirs (with permission first).

Governance Reinforcement: Weeks 4-8

3-Minute Governance Pulse (Every Session)

Governance knowledge decays without reinforcement. Starting Week 4, open every session with a 3-minute micro-activity before the opening ritual. These keep data classification and responsible use top-of-mind without adding session length.

Week 4: Rapid Classification

Read three data scenarios aloud. Each person holds up 1-4 fingers for the tier. Discuss any disagreements. Takes 2 minutes. Builds muscle memory for classification under time pressure.

Week 5: "Would You Send This?"

Show a Claude-generated output on screen (email, summary, or data table). Ask: "Would you send this as-is? What would you change?" Quick show of hands, then 60-second debrief. Reinforces Discernment as a governance behavior.

Week 6: Governance Pop Quiz

One question: "A colleague asks you to paste scholarship award amounts into Claude to draft a partner report. What do you do?" Open discussion, 2 minutes. Tests whether governance decisions are becoming instinctive.

Week 7: Teach-Back

One volunteer explains the four data tiers to the group as if teaching a new hire. Group rates the explanation. This is the highest-fidelity test of whether governance knowledge has transferred from policy to practice.

Week 8: Real Scenario Swap

Each leader brings one governance question they encountered during the pilot. Swap scenarios with a partner. Classify and discuss. This surfaces edge cases the framework hasn't addressed and feeds into the post-pilot governance review.

If the group consistently gets these right, governance has landed. If disagreements persist on Week 7-8, flag the specific scenarios for the post-pilot governance framework revision.

Leadership Module Accountability

Department / Team Leaders: Build Your Team Environment For Your Team and the Organization to Use

This pilot produces 10 leaders who shape the AI environment for their department. Each leader is responsible for defining what goes into their unit's Claude workspace: which organizational knowledge to load, which skills and projects are worth implementing, and which practices are worth streamlining. This is the capstone.

Environment Design Structure (The Scaffold)

Every leader's environment design addresses five elements:

  1. One real-work scenario from the unit's actual workflows (not a generic example)
  2. Governance tier classification for the scenario's data (public, internal, sensitive, restricted)
  3. A worked prompt example using CTCC (Context-Task-Content-Constraints)
  4. An output evaluation exercise: What's good? What needs revision? Why?
  5. A "What You CAN Do" permission list for the role (what's allowed, what's not, and why)

Timeline

  • Weeks 1–3: Use Claude yourself. Identify your unit's highest-value AI use case.
  • Weeks 4–5: Design your unit's environment. Identify the knowledge, skills, and projects your team needs. Bring questions to sessions.
  • Weeks 6–7: Peer review. Present your environment design to the cohort for feedback.
  • Week 8: Finalize. Your team inherits the intelligence layer you designed during Q1 rollout.

Facilitator Accountability

Check in on module progress every session starting Week 3. The weekly check-in (in your tracking sheet) captures this. Use this as your conversation starter:

"Where are you in your module? What's the real-work scenario you're using? What question do you have that we can solve as a group right now?"

If someone is stuck, pair them with another leader who's further along. This is peer mentorship in action and deepens psychological safety.

The Five Principles of Psychological Safety in This Pilot

  1. Struggle is signal, and signal is valuable. When something doesn't work, the cohort learns. Every failure that's shared is a data point that moves the whole group forward.
  2. Leaders model first. If you haven't shared a failure, you haven't earned the right to expect others to. This applies to Preston and champions most of all.
  3. Curiosity over judgment. The response to "I haven't used Claude this week" is "What got in the way?" and nothing else. No criticism, no impatience. Just understanding.
  4. Progress over perfection. A leader who used Claude once this week and learned something is ahead of one who used it ten times on autopilot. Learning is the metric, not volume.
  5. This pilot invests in YOU. AI fluency is a career asset that increases your value as a leader, inside DC CAP and beyond. You're building a skill that will define leadership in your organization.

Facilitation Transition Checklist

From Preston to Champions: Week by Week

Champion Facilitator Playbook

By Week 5, you are co-facilitating AI Friday sessions. By Week 7, you are leading them. This playbook gives you the structure to do both with confidence.

Session Script: 45-Minute AI Friday

0:00 - 0:05
Open with a Win

Ask: "Who used Claude on something real this week? What happened?" Let 2-3 people share. This normalizes experimentation and gives the group social proof.

0:05 - 0:10
Frame the Activity

Name the activity, state the competency it targets (which of the 4Ds), and explain what participants will walk away with. Keep this under 3 minutes. Read from the activity guide if needed.

0:10 - 0:35
Run the Activity

Use the activity guide for the specific exercise. Your job is timekeeper and question-router, not lecturer. If someone gets stuck, pair them with someone who figured it out. If the whole group is stuck, model the task live on screen.

0:35 - 0:45
Close with Reflection

Ask: "What surprised you?" and "What will you try differently next week?" Capture 2-3 responses. Remind participants to complete the weekly check-in. Preview next week's topic.

When Someone Asks Something You Cannot Answer

"Can I put [specific data type] into Claude?"

Say: "Great question. Check the data tier classification on the Start Here page. If the data is Tier 1 or Tier 2, the answer is no. If you are unsure which tier, flag it for Preston before using it."

"Claude gave me something wrong. Is this tool broken?"

Say: "This is actually a Discernment moment. Claude produces outputs that look polished but require verification. Walk us through what you asked and what it returned. Let's figure out together where the output went wrong and how to catch it next time."

"I don't see how this applies to my role."

Say: "What is a task you do at least weekly that involves writing, summarizing, or organizing information? Let's try that one live right now." Then help them build a prompt using the Description framework: Context, Task, Content, Constraints.

"What about [technical question about Claude's architecture/capabilities]?"

Say: "I want to make sure I give you the right answer. Let me capture that question and get back to you by end of day. Preston or the Innovation Hub team will have the technical detail." Then message Preston directly.

Signals to Watch For

Disengagement

Someone stops attending or goes silent during activities. Follow up 1:1 within 48 hours. Ask what would make the sessions more useful for their specific work. Report to Preston if pattern continues.

Governance Confusion

Multiple people ask the same data classification question. This signals the training material has a gap. Note the specific question and share with Preston for the next weekly check-in review.

Breakout Momentum

Someone builds a workflow that saves real time or improves real output quality. Ask them to present it at the next session. Peer demonstrations accelerate adoption faster than any training material.

Questions you cannot resolve in session: Message Preston Magouirk directly via Teams or email at preston.magouirk@dccap.org. For data security concerns, escalate immediately rather than waiting for the next session.

Peer Accountability Pairs

In Week 6, each leader pairs with one other leader from a different unit. The commitment: by the end of the pilot, each person teaches their partner one specific AI workflow they built during the program. This creates a concrete transfer mechanism and prevents post-pilot reversion.

Week 6: Pair Assignment

Assign pairs across units (Programs + Development, Data + Communications, etc.). Each person names the specific workflow they will teach. Write it down. Share with the group. Public commitment drives follow-through.

Week 8: Teach-Back in Session

During the final session, 2-3 pairs demonstrate their teach-back live. The partner explains what they learned and how they will apply it in their own unit. This is the proof that knowledge transferred beyond the individual.

June Check-In (Post-Pilot)

Two weeks after pilot close, pairs meet for 15 minutes. Each person answers: "Did you teach the workflow to someone on your team? What happened?" Results feed into the Q1 FY2027 rollout planning. If pairs report successful transfer, the workflow gets added to the organizational skill library.
