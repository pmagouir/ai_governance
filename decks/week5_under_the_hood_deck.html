Q1 · Description

What is a context window?

From the real world

A chef's mise en place.

Walk into a professional kitchen 30 minutes before service. Every station has its prep laid out — oils, salts, garnishes, sauces, proteins portioned and ready.

What's on the station is what can go into the dish in the next 90 seconds. Whatever wasn't prepped doesn't exist for that ticket.

Standard professional kitchen practice. Documented in every culinary textbook from Escoffier forward.
In your Claude work

The window is the station.

The context window holds everything you've pasted, everything Claude has said back, and any reference files loaded. Whatever's not in the window can't shape the answer.

Load names, dates, voice rules, audience, format. Treat each chat like a focused service. When the task ends, close the chat.

The takeaway: Description quality is bounded by what sits in the window.

Q2 · Description

Why does context matter?

From the real world

A doctor without your history.

A patient walks in with chest pain. With no medical history, that symptom could be heartburn, anxiety, a heart attack, or a dozen other things. The doctor guesses.

Add history — cardiac stent six months ago. Add family — father died of MI at 52. Add behavior — ran six miles before symptoms started. Each piece of context narrows the diagnosis to the one that fits.

Diagnostic accuracy correlates more strongly with quality of patient history than with imaging or labs. (Hampton et al., BMJ 1975; replicated repeatedly since.)
In your Claude work

Claude is the doctor.

"Write me an email to a parent" gets you the average of the internet. Add the named family, the specific deadline, the cycle date, the voice rules — and Claude narrows to the answer that fits DC CAP.

If you keep retyping the same context every week, that context belongs in a Project. The doctor doesn't ask for your history every time. They have the chart.

The takeaway: Empty context, generic answer. Rich context, sharp answer.

Q3 · Description carried forward

Why does memory matter?

From the real world

The B-17 and the checklist.

In 1935, Boeing's Model 299 prototype — the plane that became the B-17 — crashed on its first test flight. The most experienced pilot in the Army Air Corps had forgotten to release the elevator lock.

The Army called it "too much airplane for one man to fly." Boeing's answer wasn't to retrain pilots. They built a pre-flight checklist. The Model 299 went on to fly 1.8 million miles without an accident.

Boeing Model 299 first flight: Oct 30, 1935. Pilot: Maj. Ployer Hill. Story documented in Atul Gawande's The Checklist Manifesto (2009).
In your Claude work

A Project is the checklist.

Claude doesn't remember you across chats. Each new conversation starts fresh. Trying to make one chat remember everything is being the pilot who forgot the lock.

Memory belongs in files. A Project carries context across tasks. A Skill carries a procedure across runs. The retyping you keep doing is the procedure waiting to be written down.

The takeaway: Every Skill you write is Description carried forward.

Q4 · Diligence signal

What is model drift?

From the real world

Tay, 2016.

Microsoft launched a chatbot named Tay on Twitter on March 23, 2016. Tay was designed to learn from conversation. Within 16 hours, trolls had steered her into producing racist and offensive content. Microsoft pulled her offline.

The mechanism is the same one that happens in every long chat — quieter, slower. The longer a conversation runs, the more recent inputs dominate the original instructions. Newer signals crowd out older ones.

Microsoft Tay launch and shutdown, March 23–24, 2016. Widely covered; case study in every responsible-AI curriculum since.
In your Claude work

The chat that quietly drifted.

You correct Claude on something at message 5. By message 30, the same error is back. Tone shifts. Earlier rules dilute. That's drift.

The fix: start fresh when the trail feels off. Re-anchor inside long chats with a reminder. A Project loads the foundational context fresh every time you open a new conversation inside it.

The takeaway: When the answer thins, that's a Diligence signal — not a Claude failure.

Q5 · Delegation

What model should I use?

A chef's knife rack tells you the answer. A paring knife for delicate prep. A chef's knife for the workhorse cuts. A cleaver for breaking down bone. Same family, different jobs. Claude works the same way.

Cleaver · careful

Opus 4.6

Deep multi-step reasoning. Slowest. Most expensive.

Highest-stakes drafting. Board memos. Funder LOIs. Anything where being wrong is costly. Don't use it for routine emails or simple summaries.

Chef's knife · balanced

Sonnet 4.6

The DC CAP default. Strong reasoning, everyday speed.

Counselor emails. Partnership updates. Briefings. First drafts of grant narratives. Most Projects and most Skills. If you don't know which to pick, this is the answer.

Paring knife · fast

Haiku 4.5

Built for speed and volume.

Quick lookups. Classifications. Pulling structured data out of unstructured text. Don't use it for anything signed by a person or anything that needs voice.

The takeaway: Model selection is a Delegation decision. The right tool for the work — not the most expensive one in the rack.

Q6 · Diligence

What is hallucination?

From the real world

Mata v. Avianca, 2023.

Two New York lawyers used ChatGPT to write a personal injury brief against Avianca Airlines. ChatGPT cited six prior cases — including Varghese v. China Southern Airlines — with fabricated judge names, fabricated quotes, fabricated citation numbers.

When the lawyers asked ChatGPT if the cases were real, ChatGPT said yes and pointed to "Westlaw and LexisNexis." None of them existed. Judge P. Kevin Castel sanctioned the lawyers $5,000 in May 2023. He called the analysis "gibberish."

Mata v. Avianca, Inc., No. 1:22-cv-01461 (S.D.N.Y. 2023). Judge P. Kevin Castel, sanctions order June 22, 2023.
In your Claude work

Pattern completion, not knowledge.

Hallucination is when a model confidently states something untrue. A fabricated quote. A made-up statistic. A paper that doesn't exist. A person who never said the thing.

It happens because LLMs are pattern completers. When they don't know, they generate text that sounds like the right kind of answer. Names, numbers, dates, citations are highest-risk. Load the source. Ask for citations. Cross-check anything that lands in front of a funder, board, or partner.

The takeaway: The lawyers asked ChatGPT to verify itself. ChatGPT said yes. Don't ask the model to grade its own homework.

Q6 · Diligence — what it costs

Two more, with the bill attached.

Google · February 2023

Bard's first demo.

In its public launch ad, Google's Bard chatbot was asked what new discoveries to tell a 9-year-old about the James Webb Space Telescope. Bard said JWST "took the very first pictures" of a planet outside our solar system.

Wrong. The first exoplanet image came from the European Southern Observatory's Very Large Telescope in 2004. Reuters caught it. The story broke on the day of Google's Paris AI event.

Alphabet stock dropped 7.7%. ~$100 billion in market cap erased in 24 hours.
CNN Business, NPR, Reuters (Feb 8–9, 2023). Confirmed by NASA records and ESO archive.
Air Canada · February 2024

The bereavement-fare chatbot.

Jake Moffatt asked Air Canada's chatbot about bereavement fares after his grandmother died. The chatbot told him he could apply for a refund within 90 days of booking. Air Canada's actual policy required pre-booking with the discount applied.

Air Canada refused the refund. The British Columbia Civil Resolution Tribunal sided with Moffatt: a company is liable for negligent misrepresentation made by its chatbot. "Air Canada is responsible for all the information on its website."

Air Canada ordered to pay $812.02. Precedent set across Canadian jurisdictions.
Moffatt v. Air Canada, 2024 BCCRT 149 (Feb 14, 2024).

The takeaway: Hallucination has a market price, a legal price, and a reputational price. Verify before you let it ship.

Q7 · Diligence

How do I verify and validate?

From the real world

The pre-flight checklist + the flight plan.

A commercial pilot does two things before takeoff. The pre-flight checklist — verify every part works. Engines, controls, fuel, instruments. The flight plan — validate the route fits the mission. Weather, weight, alternate airports, fuel reserves.

Both are mandatory under FAA regulations. A plane in perfect mechanical condition flying into a thunderstorm is verified, not validated. A flawless flight plan in a plane with a broken altimeter is validated, not verified.

14 CFR § 91.103 (Preflight action) and 14 CFR § 91.213 (Inoperative instruments). FAA Aeronautical Information Manual, current edition.
In your Claude work

Two gates. Both required.

Verify — are the facts true? Names, numbers, dates, citations, claims. Cross-check anything material. Don't ask the model to grade itself.

Validate — does it fit the work? Right audience, right format, right tone, right move. Read it as the audience before you send.

For the highest-stakes work, run it through the adversarial-audit Skill from Week 4 — or hand it to a teammate. Design the gates into the workflow, not bolted on at the end.

The takeaway: Diligence is two moves, not one.

Q8 · Description

How do I write a better prompt?

From the real world

Ordering at a restaurant.

Vague: "I'd like food." The server stares at you.

Sharp: "The grilled salmon, medium, with the seasonal vegetables instead of the potatoes, dressing on the side. And the sparkling water, no lemon."

Same kitchen. Same ingredients. The order decides what arrives at the table. Specificity is not rude in a restaurant. It's the only thing that gets you what you want.

If you have ever asked a contractor to "fix the kitchen" and gotten a quote you couldn't use, you have lived this principle.
In your Claude work

Five parts.

1. State the work, not the task.
2. Anchor in concrete facts — named partners, real numbers, real dates.
3. Tell Claude the audience and the format.
4. Iterate in dialogue. Show Claude what's wrong; don't start over.
5. Save what works.

Every great prompt is a SKILL.md waiting to be written. The more reps you put in on the five-part structure, the less you have to rewrite it.

The takeaway: Description is the most valuable move in this whole pilot.

The compound

Eight mechanics. Two effects.

When we get these right, two things happen at once. Work quality goes up. The error surface drops. Every new tool we pick up from here — Skills, automations, agents — amplifies both effects.

01
Better chats
Focused windows. The right model. Drift caught early. Verification before send.
02
Better tasks
Five-part prompts. Anchored in facts. Named audience. Iteration replaces restart.
03
Better Projects
Reference files do the memory. Voice carries. Twelve runs inherit what you built once.
04
Better Skills
Procedures with verify-and-validate gates inside them. Anyone on the team runs the same move.

The new tools scale the quality when the basics are solid. They scale the errors when they're not. Same mechanism, two directions.
