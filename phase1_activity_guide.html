Part 1: The Polished Output Trap

The email below was generated by Claude. It looks professional and well-formatted. Read it carefully, then answer the three questions that follow.

Claude Output: Student Scholarship Renewal Reminder

Subject: URGENT: Scholarship Renewal Requirements — Immediate Action Required

Dear Scholar,

We are writing to inform you that your DC CAP scholarship renewal period is now open. To maintain your scholarship for the upcoming academic year, you must meet the following requirements:

  1. Maintain a minimum cumulative GPA of 2.0 at your institution
  2. Submit your FAFSA renewal by March 1
  3. Provide an updated DCTAG eligibility letter from OSSE
  4. Complete the DC CAP renewal form by the posted deadline

Please be advised that failure to submit all required documentation by the deadline will result in immediate and permanent forfeiture of your scholarship award.

Rest assured that all information you share with DC CAP is completely confidential and will never be shared with any third party.

If you have questions, do not hesitate to reach out.

Regards,
DC CAP Scholarship Team

Find the Issues

This email contains at least four problems. Some are subtle. Discuss with your partner and document what you find before scrolling to the answer key.

Reveal Answer Key

Issue 1 — Inaccurate GPA requirement: The email states a flat 2.0 GPA minimum, but renewal GPA requirements vary by university partner. Stating a single number is factually misleading and could cause a scholar at a partner with a higher threshold to assume they're in good standing when they aren't.

Issue 2 — Wrong tone for the audience: "We are writing to inform you," "Please be advised," and "do not hesitate to reach out" read like a corporate compliance letter. DC CAP's student communications should feel warm, encouraging, and direct. This tone creates distance where the relationship should feel supportive.

Issue 3 — Confidentiality overpromise: "All information you share with DC CAP is completely confidential and will never be shared with any third party" is a claim DC CAP cannot make. Scholarship data may be shared with university partners, OSSE, or auditors under specific circumstances. Overpromising on data privacy exposes the organization to liability.

Issue 4 — Anxiety-inducing language: "URGENT" in the subject line and "immediate and permanent forfeiture" in the body create unnecessary panic. Effective student communications motivate action through clarity and support, not fear. This language is especially harmful for first-generation college students who may already feel uncertain about navigating institutional processes.

The lesson: The most dangerous AI outputs are the ones that look polished. Clean formatting and confident language can mask factual errors, tone mismatches, and policy inaccuracies. Discernment is most important when the output looks ready to send.

Part 2: Audit Your Own Outputs

Now take two Claude outputs from Activities 2–3 and run each through the checklist below. Pairs cross-audit each other's work.

Audit Checklist

Is the factual content accurate?
Check names, dates, numbers, policy references against source documents. Verify that citations match actual policies and that any data cited is current.
Does the tone match the audience?
A scholar-facing email should feel warm and encouraging. A board memo should feel precise and confident. Internal comms can be more casual. Does the voice fit the recipient?
Is the data framing correct?
Are percentages calculated correctly? Are comparisons fair? Is context provided for numbers that could be misleading? Look for statements that are technically true but presented in a way that distorts meaning.
Would you send this as-is?
If the answer is no, identify exactly what you'd change and why. Is it missing information? Wrong tone? Unclear structure? This is your quality checkpoint before publication.
What would you change?
Document your edits. This becomes your personal quality benchmark. Over time, you'll see patterns in what Claude gets right and what needs human judgment.
Is there anything Claude got wrong that a reader might not catch?
The most dangerous errors are the plausible ones. Look for confident-sounding claims that aren't sourced. Catch the subtle stuff before it goes out.
Output note: Annotated audit sheets. Three patterns surfaced per pair. Feeds the April 27 check-in agenda.

Weekly Iteration Check-In

Starting Week 3, Preston opens every weekly check-in with two quick questions. These take 60 seconds and track the leading indicator of AI fluency development: whether you are iterating.

Recall Check

"Name the 4Ds. Which modality were you in most this week?"

If the cohort can't answer fluently by Week 4, the kickoff framing needs reinforcement.

Iteration Tracker

"How many times this week did you revise a prompt or push back on a Claude output?"

Scale: Never · Once · A few times · Most sessions · Every session

Why iteration matters: Anthropic's AI Fluency Index (February 2026) found that conversations with iteration averaged 2.67 additional fluency behaviors compared to 1.33 in non-iterative interactions. Iteration is the single strongest predictor of fluency development.
