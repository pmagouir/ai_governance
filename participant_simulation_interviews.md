# DC CAP Enterprise AI Leadership Pilot: Participant Simulation Interviews

**Pilot Overview:** 60-day pilot (April 6 - June 5, 2026) deploying Claude to 9 staff across 3 organizational units. 10 simulated participants spanning diverse roles, experience levels, and psychological dispositions.

---

## Participant 1: Angela Washington

**Role:** Senior Program Manager, Student Success Unit
**Tenure:** 8 years at DC CAP
**Age/Profile:** 42, manages 5 counselors, mother of 2, moderate tech comfort
**AI Experience:** Has used ChatGPT 3 times for personal budgeting, feels "mildly interested but nervous"

### A. First Impressions (Pilot Hub)

**What grabs attention:**
- The animated stats at the top ("60 days," "9 leaders," "24 skills") feels professional and official—reassuring that this is real and structured, not a one-off experiment.
- The "Getting Started" section with prerequisites feels manageable. She's relieved there's a clear checklist.

**What confuses her:**
- The "three interfaces" (Chat, Cowork, Code) mentioned in the Start Here section—she doesn't understand what "Code" means. Is she supposed to write code? That makes her anxious.
- She's not entirely clear on the difference between the Kickoff Deck and the Start Here Guide. Are these mandatory in sequence, or can she skip one?

**Emotional reaction:**
- Cautious optimism. She's been at nonprofits long enough to know that enthusiasm doesn't guarantee follow-through, but this feels intentional. The branding is polished. She's thinking: "Okay, they're serious about this. Let me see if it actually works."
- Low hum of anxiety: *Will I be good at this? Will my team think I'm abandoning human judgment?*

---

### B. Survey Experience

**How she feels:**
- The survey feels comprehensive but a bit long. She's on her lunch break and slightly rushed.
- When she gets to the AI Knowledge section with the multiple-choice questions, she pauses on "hallucination" and "context window." She's heard these terms once in the Kickoff Deck video but isn't 100% confident in her answers.
- The "I have read and understood the AI Governance Policy" checkbox makes her click carefully. She *did* read it, but she worries she's forgotten something important.

**Where she struggles:**
- The "Current AI Use" frequency items confuse her. She doesn't use AI professionally. The options feel like they assume she already does. She chooses "Never" for most items and wonders if that will look bad.
- The applied skills section (prompt rewriting, output evaluation) feels intimidating. She rewrites a weak prompt about scheduling student meetings and isn't sure if her "strong" version is actually good or just slightly less bad.

**What she thinks of results:**
- Her radar chart shows strong "Learning Orientation" and "Governance Concern" but low "Current AI Use" and "Confidence in AI."
- Her honest reaction: *This is accurate. I'm a quick learner but not a tech person. And yes, I'm worried about the governance stuff. Good.*
- She screenshots the results (standard nonprofit behavior: document everything).

---

### C. Kickoff Deck Reaction

**What resonates:**
- The opening framing about "You will write prompts that produce nothing useful" feels honest. She appreciates that the pilot isn't pretending this is easy.
- The 4D framework (Delegation, Description, Discernment, Diligence) feels immediately applicable. She's already thinking about which of her weekly tasks fit.
- The scholarship email example before/after prompt is concrete and relevant. She recognizes the DC CAP voice in the examples.

**What falls flat or feels irrelevant:**
- The "three modalities" (Automation, Augmentation, Agency) feels overly theoretical. She gets it in the abstract but wants more real examples from her actual work.
- The data governance 4-tier classification (Internal, Confidential, Student PII, Restricted) is important but there's no real workflow shown. How does she actually *apply* this when she's in Claude?

**Questions she has afterward:**
- "Can Claude see my Salesforce data, or do I have to copy-paste student info into the chat?"
- "What happens if I accidentally use an AI summary in an email to a family and they ask where it came from? How honest do I have to be?"
- "My counselors are going to ask if this means I'm replacing them. What do I say?"

---

### D. Start Here Guide Assessment

**Is this useful for her level?**
- Yes and no. It's useful as a reference, but it's dense. She reads the File Management Rules section (relevant) but skips most of the Governance theory.
- The "3 Interfaces" tab confuses her. She now understands that "Code" is for developers and she probably won't use it, but she wishes someone had just said that upfront.

**What's missing for her role:**
- **A workflow for scholarship counseling.** The guide is generic. She needs: "Here's how to use Claude when drafting a student outreach email" or "Here's how to handle a difficult family inquiry with AI help."
- **A troubleshooting section.** What if Claude gives her bad advice about policy? What if the output is technically correct but feels cold?
- **Templates or prompts for common tasks** she actually does: email drafts, meeting agendas, follow-up outreach, data summarization.

**What's overwhelming or unclear:**
- The "Responsible Use Principles" section is ethics-heavy. She agrees with it but doesn't know how to operationalize it moment-to-moment. When does "critical evaluation" become paranoia?

---

### E. Activity Guide Feedback

**Which activities does she find most valuable?**
- **Governance Walk (Activity 1):** This is immediately useful. Classifying 10 scenarios using the 4-tier framework is practical and reduces her anxiety. "Oh, I can actually *do* this. I know what student PII is."
- **Output Audit (Activity 4):** She likes the structure of evaluating Claude's work for accuracy, tone, and completeness. It gives her a critical lens that feels concrete.

**Which feel like a waste of time?**
- **Prompt Lab (Activity 2):** Writing weak and strong prompts for her own task feels artificial in a 30-minute window. She wants to try this with *real work* over a week, not in a lab setting.
- **Skill Trigger Challenge (Activity 3):** Discovering which phrases activate the 24 custom skills feels gamified. She's thinking: "When am I actually going to sit around and test 24 skills? I need to solve a problem *right now*."

**What would she change?**
- "Let me do this with a real task from my inbox. Not a practice scenario."
- "More time for the Governance Walk. People need to talk through edge cases, not just classify scenarios."
- "A follow-up workshop where we try these activities with actual work and debrief together. One-off activities don't stick."

---

### F. Overall Assessment

**Top 1-2 things she LOVES:**
1. The 4D framework—it gives her permission to think systematically about what *should* go to AI.
2. The focus on governance. She was expecting a "move fast and break things" AI rollout. This feels responsible.

**Top 1-2 things she finds CONFUSING:**
1. What happens when Claude gives bad advice? Who's responsible? (The framework says *she* is, but it feels scary.)
2. How to actually integrate this into daily work—the gap between "here's the framework" and "I'm now sitting at my desk and need to make a decision in 2 minutes."

**Top 1-2 things she DISLIKES or would change:**
1. The survey and kickoff feel disconnected from real application. More time in the Phase 1 activities or even earlier hands-on time.
2. No clear pathway for "What if I mess up?" She's imagining a scenario where she uses AI output that turns out to be wrong and it affects a student.

**Honest "hallway conversation" quote:**
> "I think this is real and probably good, but I'm waiting to see if it actually works for me. The framework is solid, but executing it while managing 5 people and 100 open tasks—I don't know. I'm going to try it because I have to, but I'm nervous about the gap between the training and Tuesday morning when I have 47 emails."

## Participant 2: Marcus Chen

**Role:** Data Analyst, Data & Technology Unit
**Tenure:** 3 years at DC CAP
**Age/Profile:** 29, highly technical, writes R, skeptical of hype
**AI Experience:** Tried ChatGPT for code debugging, found it "mediocre," uses it rarely

### A. First Impressions (Pilot Hub)

**What grabs attention:**
- The infrastructure. He immediately checks for a tech stack: What's this built with? (HTML, CSS, vanilla JS—reasonable.)
- The animated stats feel decorative to him. He's looking for the *actual* resources and the substance.

**What confuses him:**
- The "24 custom skills" claim on the hub makes him skeptical. Custom built how? Where's the documentation?
- Why is there a "Daily Briefing newsletter"? That sounds like noise.

**Emotional reaction:**
- Mild cynicism mixed with intellectual curiosity. He's thinking: *Okay, let's see if they actually know what they're talking about.*
- He has the energy of someone who knows his domain well and is watching to see if a new tool respects that knowledge or trivializes it.

---

### B. Survey Experience

**How he feels:**
- The survey is well-designed. He appreciates the structure and the attempt to measure construct validity.
- He breezes through most items quickly—he's confident in his answers.

**Where he struggles:**
- The "AI Knowledge" section on "hallucinations" and "context windows" is too simple for him. He wants to know about token limits, temperature, training data cutoff dates. The multiple-choice options feel dumbed down.
- The applied skills section asks him to rewrite a prompt about "scheduling student meetings." He doesn't do that kind of work. The example doesn't translate to his domain (data analysis, R scripts, SQL queries).

**What he thinks of results:**
- His radar chart is skewed: high "AI Knowledge," high "Current AI Use," but moderate "Learning Orientation" (he thinks he knows most of what he needs).
- His reaction: *The framework is standard AI literacy stuff. I'm not sure what this pilot is going to teach me that I haven't figured out on my own.*
- He's not defensive, just skeptical. This is his baseline posture.

---

### C. Kickoff Deck Reaction

**What resonates:**
- The explicit acknowledgment that AI isn't a replacement for expertise. "Claude drafted it. You own it." He appreciates this framing.
- The 4D framework has utility, even if it's not novel to him. Delegation, Description, Discernment, Diligence—these map to his existing code review practices.

**What falls flat or feels irrelevant:**
- The scholarship email examples feel soft. He's thinking: "Why is every example about communication and outreach? What about data validation, query optimization, or analysis?"
- The "three modalities" (Automation, Augmentation, Agency) feel like marketing language to him.

**Questions he has afterward:**
- "Can I feed Claude an actual SQL query and have it optimize it, or is this tool designed for people who can't code?"
- "What about data security? The governance framework talks about 4 tiers, but what's the actual risk if I accidentally paste anonymized (but theoretically identifiable) scholar data?"
- "How do I validate Claude's statistical advice? It hallucinates with stats sometimes."

---

### D. Start Here Guide Assessment

**Is this useful for his level?**
- Partially. The governance section is useful (he's always thinking about data security). The Responsible Use Principles section is too abstract for him.
- He doesn't need the "Interfaces" explainer. He's going to use Chat and maybe Cowork. He knows he won't use Code features because he writes in R, not in Claude's code environment.

**What's missing for his role:**
- **Technical guidance for analysts.** How do you use Claude to help with data validation, documentation, statistical framing?
- **Examples from his work.** The guide is entirely nonprofit operations examples. Nothing about analytics, dashboards, reproducible research.
- **Limitations and guardrails specific to technical work.** When Claude gives you code, how do you review it? What are the most dangerous hallucination patterns in technical contexts?

**What's overwhelming or unclear:**
- The governance framework is solid, but he's waiting for the technical implementation details. How do you actually prevent PII leakage in practice? (The answer: you have to be very careful. The guide doesn't give him that security.)

---

### E. Activity Guide Feedback

**Which activities does he find most valuable?**
- **Governance Walk (Activity 1):** He appreciates the rigor. It's a good forcing function to think systematically about data classification.
- **Output Audit (Activity 4):** If the outputs are from his domain (data work), this would be useful. But the examples are generic nonprofit outputs.

**Which feel like a waste of time?**
- **Prompt Lab (Activity 2):** He's already an expert at writing prompts for coding tasks. Rewriting a prompt about student meetings feels condescending.
- **Skill Trigger Challenge (Activity 3):** This is gamification of a lookup process. He'd rather have a list of 24 skills with descriptions and example triggers.

**What would he change?**
- "Give me domain-specific activities. One for analysts, one for program staff, one for finance. Not one-size-fits-all."
- "More technical rigor on the Output Audit. Show me examples where Claude's code is subtly wrong but plausible-looking. That's the real risk."
- "Let me bring a real data task and work through the 4D framework with it, not a practice scenario."

---

### F. Overall Assessment

**Top 1-2 things he LOVES:**
1. The governance framework—it's necessary and well-thought-out.
2. The honesty that this is a 60-day experiment. He respects that it's not oversold as "AI will fix everything."

**Top 1-2 things he finds CONFUSING:**
1. How domain-specific this can be. The pilot feels designed for general nonprofit users, not for specialists like him.
2. The actual risk/reward calculation for technical work—when is Claude genuinely helpful vs. when is it cargo culting?

**Top 1-2 things he DISLIKES or would change:**
1. The activities feel designed for people with no AI exposure. He's not the target audience, and it shows.
2. The lack of technical depth. He wants to see failure cases, edge cases, and honest limitations specific to data work.

**Honest "hallway conversation" quote:**
> "The governance stuff is solid. I'll use Claude for some work, probably for documentation and refactoring existing code. But I'm not sure this pilot is designed for people who actually know technical stuff. The framework is one size fits all. I'll figure out what works for my team and ignore the rest."
