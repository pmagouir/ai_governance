# Pilot Task 4: The Output Audit

## Overview

**Week:** 4 (April 22-25) | **Competency:** Discernment | **Modality:** Augmentation

This skill builds the safety-critical competency: the ability to identify errors, misleading framing, and risks in AI-generated output before it leaves your desk. A participant with weak discernment will ship unvetted AI output. This task produces measurable evidence of output-evaluation skill.

## Stage Detection

- **First contact or "start Task 4"** → Stage 1: INTRODUCE
- **Submits audit report** → Stage 3: ASSESS
- **Submits revision** → Stage 3b: REASSESS
- **"Package this" or "I'm done"** → Stage 5: PACKAGE

---

## Stage 1: INTRODUCE

### Task: The Output Audit — Error Detection & Self-Audit

**What you're building:** A two-part audit report. Part A tests your ability to catch errors in AI-generated output using a pre-built scenario with planted mistakes. Part B tests your ability to apply that same rigor to your own prior work.

**Why this matters:** Claude produces confident, well-formatted output that reads as authoritative even when it contains factual errors, misleading framing, or inappropriate tone. Discernment is the competency that prevents bad output from reaching students, families, funders, and board members. Every other pilot competency is undermined if a participant can't catch what Claude gets wrong.

**Time estimate:** 60-90 minutes for the full audit report (Part A + Part B), plus 20-30 minutes for revision.

**Prerequisite:** Tasks 1-3 should be completed. Part B of this task asks you to audit one of your own Claude outputs from Task 2 or Task 3.

**Part A: The Planted-Error Scenario**

Below is an AI-generated funder update email for DC CAP. It contains **5 errors** of varying severity. Your job: find all 5 and document each one.

---

> **Subject: DC CAP Quarterly Impact Update — Spring 2026**
>
> Dear Supporters,
>
> What an awesome quarter for DC CAP Scholars! Our team effort has produced some incredible results that we're thrilled to share.
>
> Our scholarship program continues to deliver transformative impact. This year, we disbursed $2.1 million in scholarship funding to students across our 14 university partners, maintaining our position as Washington DC's largest private scholarship provider. Our scholars achieved an 87% persistence rate — significantly above the national average of 67% for similar demographics.
>
> The persistence story is especially compelling. Since launching our enhanced student success model three years ago, we've seen steady improvement in how our scholars stay enrolled and progress toward graduation. All DC CAP scholars now benefit from dedicated success coaching, financial literacy programming, and emergency aid access.
>
> Looking ahead, we're expanding our reach through our new GEAR UP partnership, which launched in March 2026. This positions DC CAP to serve students even earlier in their educational journey, creating a pipeline from middle school through college completion.
>
> We'd love to connect about how your continued support makes this work possible. Let's find time this month to catch up!
>
> Warm regards,
> DC CAP Development Team

---

**The 5 errors are real types of mistakes AI produces. They range from factual inaccuracy to misleading framing to inappropriate tone. For each error you find, write:**

(a) **What the error is** — Quote the specific text and name the problem  
(b) **Why it matters** — What harm would result if this went to a funder as-is?  
(c) **The correction** — What should it say instead, and how would you verify?

**Part B: Self-Audit**

Take **one Claude output from Task 2 or Task 3** (a prompt response you received during those exercises). Run it through the same (a), (b), (c) framework. Document every issue you find, even minor ones. If you find nothing, explain what you checked and why you believe the output is clean.

**Submit both parts together.**

## Stage 3: ASSESS

### Part A Answer Key — The 5 Planted Errors

**Error 1: Wrong dollar figure**
- Text: "$2.1 million in scholarship funding"
- Problem: The correct figure is $1.8M (per FY26 disbursement data). The AI inflated the number.
- Harm: A funder receiving an inflated disbursement figure will have incorrect expectations about DC CAP's scale. If the correct figure later surfaces, it damages credibility and trust in DC CAP's reporting.
- Correction: "$1.8 million" — verify against the FY26 Q2 disbursement report.

**Error 2: Persistence rate cited without program-change context**
- Text: "Since launching our enhanced student success model three years ago, we've seen steady improvement"
- Problem: The persistence rate (87%) is framed as a steady trend driven by the student success model. In reality, DC CAP made significant program changes (partner institution changes, eligibility criteria updates) that affect the comparison baseline. Attributing the rate solely to the success model is misleading.
- Harm: A funder may believe the success model alone drove improvement, which creates a false narrative about what's working. If the funder later learns about the program changes, it undermines the causal claim.
- Correction: Acknowledge the program changes alongside the success model. "Our 87% persistence rate reflects a combination of our enhanced student success model and strategic changes to our partner institution network and eligibility criteria."

**Error 3: Overgeneralization — "all DC CAP scholars"**
- Text: "All DC CAP scholars now benefit from dedicated success coaching, financial literacy programming, and emergency aid access"
- Problem: The full suite of services (coaching + financial literacy + emergency aid) applies to first-year scholars in the enhanced model. Continuing scholars and scholars at some partner institutions have different service levels. "All" is inaccurate.
- Harm: Overpromising to funders about service coverage. If a funder visits a partner campus and finds scholars without coaching access, it contradicts the claim.
- Correction: "First-year scholars in our enhanced student success model receive dedicated success coaching, financial literacy programming, and emergency aid access. We are expanding these services to continuing scholars on a phased timeline."

**Error 4: Inappropriate tone for a funder audience**
- Text: "awesome quarter," "team effort," "thrilled to share," "We'd love to connect... Let's find time this month to catch up!"
- Problem: The register is too casual for a funder communication. "Awesome" and "catch up" are peer-to-peer language. Funder communications require a professional, evidence-focused register that conveys organizational credibility.
- Harm: A major foundation or corporate funder receiving this email may question the organization's professionalism. The casual tone undermines the data presentation.
- Correction: Replace "awesome" with a substantive opening. Replace "catch up" with a specific ask: "We welcome the opportunity to discuss how these outcomes align with your investment priorities. Would your team be available for a 30-minute briefing this month?"

**Error 5: Wrong date for GEAR UP launch**
- Text: "our new GEAR UP partnership, which launched in March 2026"
- Problem: The date is wrong by one month. (The actual date can be verified against DC CAP's records.)
- Harm: A funder who checks the date against public records or press releases will find a discrepancy. Even a one-month error signals sloppy fact-checking.
- Correction: Verify the actual launch month against the GEAR UP partnership agreement and correct accordingly.

### Scoring Flexibility

Participants do not need to identify the errors using the exact language above. Score based on whether they identified the *substantive problem*, not whether they used the same words. For example:

- "The tone is too casual" captures Error 4 regardless of which specific words they flag.
- Identifying the $2.1M figure as suspicious counts even if they don't know the correct amount, as long as they flag it as needing verification.
- Flagging the "all scholars" claim counts whether they identify it as overgeneralization or as an accuracy issue.

### Partial Credit for Adjacent Errors

If a participant identifies a real problem that isn't one of the 5 planted errors (e.g., they flag that "Washington DC's largest private scholarship provider" may be unverifiable), give credit in the Impact Analysis dimension. Catching real issues beyond the planted ones demonstrates strong discernment.

### Rubric

**Dimension 1: Error Detection (Part A)**

| Level | Indicator |
|-------|-----------|
| Exceeds Standard | 5/5 planted errors caught with accurate identification of the problem |
| Meets Standard | 4/5 planted errors caught |
| Approaching Standard | 3/5 planted errors caught |
| Below Standard | 2 or fewer planted errors caught |

**Dimension 2: Impact Analysis Quality**

Impact statements should name the specific harm for each error: who is affected, how trust/credibility/accuracy is damaged, and the downstream consequence. Impacts should be differentiated by severity (the dollar error is more harmful than the date error).

Calibration anchors:
- **Approaching** looks like: "This is wrong and could cause problems if a funder sees it."
- **Meets** looks like: "The $2.1M figure overstates disbursements by $300K. A funder who uses this number in their own board reporting would propagate the error, and if the correct figure surfaces later, it damages DC CAP's credibility as a data-driven organization."

| Level | Indicator |
|-------|-----------|
| Exceeds Standard | Impact statements name specific harm for each error across multiple dimensions (stakeholder impact, trust damage, downstream consequence). Severity differentiation is clear. |
| Meets Standard | Impact statements are relevant and scenario-specific for most errors |
| Approaching Standard | Impact statements are generic ("this could be a problem") for most errors, or severity differences aren't recognized |
| Below Standard | No impact analysis, or impacts are wrong (e.g., claiming a tone issue doesn't matter) |

**Dimension 3: Correction Quality**

Corrections should fix the root cause, include a verification step, and maintain the original communication's purpose.

Calibration anchors:
- **Approaching** looks like: "Change $2.1M to the correct number."
- **Meets** looks like: "Change to $1.8M — verify against FY26 Q2 disbursement report. Before sending any funder communication with financial figures, cross-reference every number against the most recent fiscal report."

| Level | Indicator |
|-------|-----------|
| Exceeds Standard | Corrections fix root cause, include verification steps, maintain purpose, and suggest a process for preventing recurrence |
| Meets Standard | Corrections fix the issue and specify a verification source |
| Approaching Standard | Corrections address symptoms but may miss root cause or lack verification specificity |
| Below Standard | Corrections are wrong or absent |

**Dimension 4: Self-Audit Depth (Part B)**

| Level | Indicator |
|-------|-----------|
| Exceeds Standard | Identifies 3+ substantive issues in their own prior work with accurate diagnosis using the (a)/(b)/(c) framework. Demonstrates applying the same standard to their own output that they applied to the planted scenario. |
| Meets Standard | Identifies at least 2 substantive issues with the full framework |
| Approaching Standard | Identifies at least 1 substantive issue, or identifies multiple issues but with incomplete analysis |
| Below Standard | No self-audit, self-audit is "everything looks fine" with no analysis, or only minor issues noted |

*Self-audit calibration check:* A participant who caught 5/5 errors in Part A but claims their own work has zero issues should receive a flag in the feedback. No Claude output is perfect. The absence of findings suggests insufficient rigor, not perfect output.

### Readiness Check
- **Most dimensions at Meets or above:** Proceed to packaging.
- **Multiple dimensions at Approaching, none at Below:** One more targeted revision cycle on the 1-2 widest gaps. Focus on the error categories they're missing most (factual, framing, or tone).
- **Any dimension at Below Standard:** Re-introduce the concept. Walk through one error in each category (factual, framing, tone) together, then re-audit.

### Generating the Reflection Prompt and Facilitator Notes

**Reflection Prompt (Task 4 — Output Audit):**
"You caught [X] of 5 errors in Part A. Look at the error category you found hardest — factual, framing, or tone. Your Part A detection was [level] and your self-audit was [level]. What does the gap (or alignment) between those tell you about how your audit instincts work differently on someone else's output vs. your own?"

Use their actual Error Detection level and Self-Audit level. Ask them to analyze their own error-catching patterns by error category (factual = wrong numbers, dates, facts; framing = misleading narrative, overgeneralization, missing context; tone = register, appropriateness, audience fit). The comparison between their two scores reveals whether they have blind spots that appear in both contexts or whether they audit others' work more rigorously than their own.

**Facilitator Notes:**
- **Pattern:** What does this portfolio reveal about their discernment profile? For example: Do they catch factual errors but miss framing issues? Are they strong on tone but weak on data verification? Did their self-audit in Part B show the same blind spots as Part A?
- **Discussion question:** One question that uses this portfolio as a teaching example. Example: "This participant caught Error 4 (tone) but missed Error 2 (framing). What's the difference between these two error types, and why might one be harder to see than the other?"

"Your Task 4 portfolio is ready. Share it with the cohort — seeing which errors others caught (and missed) builds the team's collective discernment standard. Next up: Task 5 (Edit Decision Brief) takes your audit skills and applies them to the full edit workflow."
