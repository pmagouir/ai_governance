## SECTION 2: Participant-Surfaced Gaps

**Data source:** 10 simulated participant interviews, April 3, 2026, across 3 DC CAP organizational units

### TOP THEMES (Emergent Patterns)

**Theme 1: Framework-Execution Gap (8/10 participants)**
- Participants understand the 4D framework (Delegation, Description, Discernment, Diligence) conceptually
- When asked "How would you use this for [actual work task]?" most struggle to translate frameworks into practice
- **Implication:** Activities are abstract enough to confuse application; participants need worked examples tied to DC CAP workflows
- **Evidence:** Angela (program manager), Marcus (data analyst), Robert (finance officer) all reported "sounds good in theory, not sure how to start"

**Theme 2: Domain Specificity Gap (9/10 participants)**
- Site feels designed for general nonprofit operations; specialized roles (finance, program management, development) feel underserved
- Marcus (data analyst): "The examples are email drafts and meeting summaries. What about analyzing spreadsheets or spotting data errors?"
- Robert (finance officer): "How does this work with compliance? What can I ask Claude about budget assumptions?"
- Keisha (development director): "What are safe ways to brainstorm messaging without exposing real donor data?"
- **Implication:** Without domain-specific use cases, participants assume the tool isn't relevant to them
- **Evidence:** Marcus expressed skepticism about relevance; Keisha moved cautiously; Robert focused on risk avoidance

**Theme 3: Real Work vs. Practice Scenarios (10/10 participants)**
- Every participant expressed frustration at practicing with toy examples
- Quote consensus: "I need to try this with actual [my job task], with actual stakes, to understand if it helps"
- Practice activities feel disconnected from day-to-day work
- **Implication:** Phase 1 activities are sufficient to build vocabulary but insufficient to build conviction or real behavior change
- **Evidence:** Aaliyah (advanced user) was bored by practice scenarios; Angela wanted to start with actual program descriptions; David wanted to integrate with actual file systems

**Theme 4: Governance vs. Confidence Gap (7/10 participants)**
- Participants understand "what I can't do" (governance restrictions) but are less clear on "what I can actually do safely"
- Robert (finance): "I get the tier system, but am I allowed to ask questions about budget forecasts? What's the test?"
- Marcus (data): "I know PII is forbidden. But what about de-identified data? What counts as 'de-identified'?"
- **Implication:** Governance documentation is thorough but creates paralysis; participants need positive permission ("Here's what you CAN do") not just restriction ("Here's what you CAN'T")
- **Evidence:** Robert expressed caution and risk focus; Marcus asked clarifying questions; Denise avoided engagement out of confusion

**Theme 5: Tech Jargon & Accessibility (6/10 participants)**
- Terms like "context window," "hallucination," "prompt engineering," "system prompt" appear in materials without definition
- Denise (tech-averse): "What does 'context window' mean? Is that about the window on my screen?"
- Angela: "Is 'hallucination' something I did wrong or something Claude did?"
- **Implication:** Materials assume too much baseline AI literacy; 30-40% of cohort lacks fluency with technical vocabulary
- **Evidence:** Denise, Angela, Robert, David all expressed confusion; only Keisha and Aaliyah moved past terminology quickly

**Theme 6: Voice & Authenticity Concerns (4/10 participants)**
- James (communications officer) explicitly worried: "Will DC CAP's voice disappear if Claude writes for us?"
- Keisha (development) wondered: "Can I use Claude for donor communications without it sounding generic?"
- **Implication:** Writing-adjacent roles fear AI outputs will sound corporate, not mission-aligned
- **Evidence:** James listed this as critical concern; Keisha expressed reservations; materials don't address voice preservation strategies

**Theme 7: Support & Handholding Needs (5/10 participants)**
- Denise (tech-averse, anxious) needs explicit buddy system and 1:1 check-ins
- Robert (controls-focused) needs compliance walkthroughs with examples
- Angela (capable but overwhelmed) needs permission to start small
- **Implication:** One-size-fits-all training insufficient; differentiated support pathways needed
- **Evidence:** Denise is at risk of disengagement; Robert needs finance-specific guidance; Angela needs scaffolding

**Theme 8: Success Metrics & "What Sticks" Confusion (5/10 participants)**
- The "What Sticks" recall check (vocabulary retention) feels superficial
- Marcus: "If I can name the 4Ds, have I learned anything useful?"
- Angela: "How will I know if this is actually helping?"
- **Implication:** Participants want to see evidence they're improving, not just that they remember framework names
- **Evidence:** Marcus dismissive; Angela unsure of progress; Denise worried about evaluation

**Theme 9: Peer Learning & Champion Readiness (3/10 participants raised as concern)**

## SECTION 3: Critical Gaps (Must Fix Before April 6 Launch)

### GAP 1: Missing Pre/Post Fluency Measurement Instrument (CRITICAL)

**What's Missing:**
No systematic pre/post assessment to measure AI fluency development, only vocabulary recall ("Can you name the 4Ds?"). Current site has no instrument to track engagement KPIs (active users), proficiency KPIs (fluency behaviors), or business impact KPIs (time saved, quality improvements).

**Why It Matters (Research Evidence):**
- Enterprise AI deployment research shows measurement is the foundation for evidence-based iteration and board reporting
- Anthropic's AI Fluency Index identifies 11 observable fluency behaviors (iteration, questioning, verification, etc.) that are directly measurable
- McKinsey and BCG research emphasize 3-layer measurement: engagement (30-60 days), proficiency (60-90 days), business impact (90-180 days)
- DC CAP's June board briefing and Q1 scaling decision will require quantitative evidence of fluency development and impact
- Benchmark: 60%+ monthly active users by 90 days; 75%+ by 6 months (research consensus threshold)

**Participant Impact:**
- Marcus, Angela, and others expressed frustration with superficial "What Sticks" vocabulary checks
- Participants want evidence of their own progress ("How will I know if I'm getting better?")
- Without pre/post measurement, no way to show board that fluency improved or that time was invested wisely

**Recommended Fix:**
Build a pre/post survey instrument (administered April 3-5 and June 2-5) that measures:
- **AI Orientation** (attitudes, confidence, perceived value): 5-7 Likert items
- **Learning Orientation** (growth mindset about AI skills): 4-5 items
- **Current AI Use** (baseline frequency, tools, comfort): 5-6 items
- **AI Knowledge** (conceptual understanding of 4Ds, hallucination, context window): 5-6 items
- **Applied Skills** (short scenario-based tasks): 2-3 worked examples where participant writes a prompt and evaluates output quality

Expected output: Individual scores and cohort-level radar charts showing change across 5 dimensions. Aggregate cohort report suitable for June board briefing.

**Who Should Implement:**
- Preston (design instrument, administer, analyze) with DC CAP's data interpreter for analysis

**Deadline:** April 3, 5pm (before April 6 launch; must collect baseline immediately)

**Relevant Deliverable:** ai_fluency_prelaunch_survey.html (currently exists; needs to be expanded from vocabulary checks to comprehensive instrument)

### GAP 2: No Explicit Psychological Safety Norm in Kickoff Messaging (CRITICAL for Culture)

**What's Missing:**
Kickoff deck (Slides 1-8) establishes professional framing and frameworks but does not explicitly normalize struggle, failed prompts, or iterative learning as expected and valued behaviors. Research emphasizes that psychological safety for experimentation is the strongest cultural predictor of AI adoption success, and safety norms should be explicitly stated at kickoff.

**Why It Matters (Research Evidence):**
- BCG and HBR research shows psychological safety for experimentation is foundational; without it, teams retreat into silence and stop learning
- Anthropic's enterprise adoption guidance emphasizes that growth mindset principle (permission to fail) must be established before any hands-on work
- 52% of U.S. workers worry about AI impact on jobs; fear is rational, not irrational—leadership must explicitly address it
- Evidence: Psychology Today research shows trust in direct managers and psychological safety are the strongest predictors of AI adoption engagement
- Denise, Angela, Robert, Marcus, David, and James all expressed anxiety about doing it "right" on first try, indicating safety norm is not yet established

**Participant Impact:**
- 7/10 participants exhibited anxiety about making mistakes or judgment from peers
- Denise (tech-averse) is at risk of disengagement if she feels judged for struggling
- Angela expressed worry: "Will my team think I'm abandoning human judgment?" (fear isn't addressed in materials)
- Marcus showed skepticism, partly because he doesn't see permission to experiment without "winning" immediately
- Without safety norm, participants may comply with training but not engage in the iterative learning that builds fluency

**Recommended Fix:**
Add explicit psychological safety language to kickoff deck, either in Slide 2 (Context Block) or as new interstitial between Context and 4Ds:

**Suggested Language:**
"Over the next 60 days, you will write prompts that don't work. You will get outputs that miss the mark. You will discover Claude doesn't understand your request. That is the learning process, and it's exactly what we expect. Every bad prompt teaches something. Every confusing output builds your understanding. This is not a test you'll pass or fail. It's an exploration where struggle is the point. We want to see your messy thinking, your questions, your pushback on outputs that don't fit DC CAP's voice or values. That's how AI fluency develops."

Also address job security fear explicitly:
"You might be wondering: Is this about replacing my work? No. This is about supporting you in doing more of what only humans can do—judgment, relationship, mission alignment—and less of what takes time away from that. If Claude can handle a draft, you handle the strategy."

**Who Should Implement:**
- Preston (Kickoff Deck, Slide 2 or new interstitial) + leadership (review for tone/authenticity)

**Deadline:** April 2, 5pm (before kickoff practice; final slide check before April 6)

**Relevant Deliverable:** kickoff_deck.html

4. **Communications/Voice Module** (James's need):

### GAP 5: No Real-Work Transition or Workflow Redesign Implementation Plan (BLOCKS IMPACT)

**What's Missing:**
Activities guide provides practice scenarios, but there is no explicit plan for transitioning participants from practice to actual work. No guidance on how to redesign existing workflows to use Claude (vs. just layering Claude on top of old processes).

**Why It Matters (Research Evidence):**
- Every participant in simulation expressed frustration at practicing with toy examples; all wanted to try this with actual work
- BCG research: "Full automation slowed teams by 17.7%; targeted augmentation increased performance by 24.3%"
- McKinsey: "Most companies layer AI onto old workflows and wonder why productivity stalls; technology only accelerates when workflows do"
- SoluLab: "If your AI project hasn't touched a real user in 90 days, it probably never will"
- Participant consensus: "I need to try this with actual [my job task], with actual stakes, to understand if it helps"

**Participant Impact:**
- Angela (program manager): Wants to start with actual program descriptions
- David (infrastructure): Wants to integrate with actual systems and files
- All 10 participants expressed desire to move from practice to real work
- Without real-work transition, participants may complete Phase 1 but not continue using Claude independently after pilot

**Recommended Fix:**
Design a "Real-Work Transition" module (introduce in Phase 1, Week 2; implement Weeks 3-4):

**Week 2 (Transition Planning):**
- Each participant identifies one actual work task to attempt with Claude (must be low-risk, high-frequency task)
- Examples: email drafting, meeting summary, slide outline, data analysis, message brainstorm
- Map task to governance tier (ensure it's safe)
- Identify peer reviewer or champion who will audit output

**Week 3-4 (Real-Work Execution):**
- Participant attempts actual task with Claude (e.g., drafts email response using Claude)
- Peer reviewer or champion audits output for quality, accuracy, DC CAP voice/values
- Shared learning: "Here's what worked, here's what didn't, here's what we learned"
- Document the actual workflow: "Old process → New process with Claude"

**Workflow Redesign Principles:**
- Don't just add Claude to existing workflow; redesign the workflow
- Example: Old: "Angela drafts program update email (2 hours) → Sends to communications for review (1 hour) → Sends to leadership (30 min)."
- New: "Angela requests Claude draft (10 min) → Reviews and edits to match DC CAP voice (30 min) → Sends to communications for quick review (15 min) → Sends to leadership (15 min)."
- Show the time savings; make the workflow change visible

**Documentation:**
- Create a "Workflow Redesign Template" that captures: old process → new process with Claude → time saved → quality impact
- Share examples across cohort: "Here's how Finance used Claude to speed up budget review; here's the template if you want to adapt it"

**Who Should Implement:**
- Preston (design Transition module) + champions (facilitate real-work projects with peer oversight)

**Deadline:** Week 2 of Phase 1 (April 15) for planning; Week 3-4 for execution

**Relevant Deliverables:** phase1_activity_guide.html (add Transition module); create "Workflow Redesign Template" as supplementary resource

### GAP 7: No "Polished Output" Discernment Trap in Activity 4 (BLOCKS CRITICAL FLUENCY SKILL)

**What's Missing:**
Activity 4 (The Output Audit) asks participants to audit "two Claude outputs generated during Activities 2-3." However, outputs from practice activities are often rough and clearly improvable. Anthropic's AI Fluency Index reveals that users are significantly less likely to question outputs that look polished and professional, even if they contain subtle errors.

**Why It Matters (Research Evidence):**
- Anthropic research: "Polished output paradox—users are more critical of rough outputs and less critical of outputs that look finished"
- Discernment is one of the 4Ds; it's critical for safe, responsible AI use
- Missing the polished-output trap during pilot means participants may miss errors in real work
- Every participant should encounter the challenge: "Output looks good; what's actually wrong?"

**Participant Impact:**
- Without experiencing this trap in Phase 1, participants will encounter it in real work (e.g., budget analysis that looks professional but is wrong; communications that look on-brand but have tone mismatches)
- Crysta, Aaliyah, and others who skip the trap may develop false confidence in output quality
- James (communications) won't learn to scrutinize Claude outputs for voice/authenticity if outputs look polished

**Recommended Fix:**
Add one pre-built "polished output" scenario to Activity 4:

**Activity 4 Enhancement: Polished Output Audit**
1. Provide a sample Claude-generated output that:
   - Looks professionally formatted
   - Uses correct grammar and structure

     - Plausible but incorrect statistic (e.g., "According to recent studies, 40% of first-gen students transfer" - actually 35%)
     - Tone mismatch for stated audience (e.g., output is too casual for a funder email)
     - Data framing issue (e.g., presents data as "positive" when DC CAP's context suggests caution)

2. All participants evaluate this output as part of Activity 4
3. Debrief: "Did you catch the errors? Here's why they matter. Here's how to spot them in future outputs."

"Polished Output Audit: Draft Funder Email

Supposed context: You're drafting an email update to a foundation funder about Scholar retention. Claude produces this:

[Sample professional-looking email]

Your task: Find 3 errors or concerns in this output, even though it looks polished.

(Reveal: Statistic is slightly wrong, tone is too optimistic given actual retention challenges, data presentation omits context about program changes that may affect next cohort)"
