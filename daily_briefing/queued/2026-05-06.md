## Why this fits Wednesday governance

Section 7.2 of DC CAP's framework holds staff accountable for the quality and accuracy of any AI-assisted work product. Section 7.3 requires staff authorship on all external communications. Mollick's argument supplies the empirical backing: the people who get useful work out of AI are the ones who can specify the goal, evaluate the output, and give feedback when it's wrong — the same management discipline DC CAP's framework expects. Frame this as the empirical case behind the framework's accountability rules.

## Headline claims (all VERIFIED from the article)

- Mollick taught a 4-day Wharton EMBA class where students built startup prototypes using Claude Code, Google Antigravity, ChatGPT, Claude, and Gemini. Output was an order of magnitude beyond what a full pre-AI semester used to produce.
- Three variables drive the delegate-or-do-it-yourself decision: Human Baseline Time, Probability of Success, AI Process Time. ("Equation of Agentic Work.")
- OpenAI's GDPval study: experts took ~7 hours per task; AI took minutes; expert checking took ~1 hour. GPT-5.2 Thinking and Pro models tied or beat human experts 72% of the time.
- Three management moves raise probability of success and lower process time: clearer instructions, better evaluation/feedback, easier success-checking.
- "If you can explain what you need, give effective feedback, and design ways of evaluating work, you are going to be able to work with agents."
- "What's scarce is knowing what to ask for."
- "The skills that are so often dismissed as 'soft' turned out to be the hard ones."
- "The people who thrive will be the ones who know what good looks like — and can explain it clearly enough that even an AI can deliver it."
