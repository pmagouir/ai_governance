§01 · What it does

Four tabs. One audience: the CEO and the Board.

The tool answers a small set of high-stakes questions: how much do we need to raise this year, how fast is the endowment growing or shrinking, what happens in a bad market, and what does the trajectory look like through FY35.

FY27 Pipeline

Floor · Base · Stretch tier-based revenue forecast, live from the Development Office's FY27 DC CAP Revenue Planning Tool workbook. Refreshed weekly. Shows what's pre-booked (multi-year pledges releasing now), what's new (must close in FY27), and what sits in cultivation.

Scenario Overview

Three-scenario deterministic projection (Base / Optimistic / Pessimistic) showing the operating P&L and endowment balance evolution FY27 → FY35. The Pessimistic scenario does the stress-test work — what happens in a bad market — without the analytical overhead of stochastic simulation.

Philanthropic Revenue

Stacked-bar decomposition of operating revenue by year — Committed · Likely / Renewals · Gap — with the operating revenue target as a reference line and a % secured indicator per year.

Audience-first design

The default landing is the FY27 Pipeline tab — the most operationally urgent view. Analyst depth (Scenario Overview, Philanthropic decomposition) sits behind the same nav, equally available but not in the CEO's face. Inputs panel can be collapsed. A prior Monte Carlo Stress Test tab was retired post-audit on 2026-05-13; the deterministic Pessimistic scenario carries the floor-crossing story for a $100M nonprofit board without the analytical complexity board members didn't engage with.

§02 · The math

The endowment draw is sized to need — the rate is what falls out.

DC CAP doesn't crank a "draw rate" knob each year. Finance approves a budget — scholarships, operating expenses, expected operating revenue — and the endowment draw is what's needed to balance it. The implied policy rate is a diagnostic, not a lever.

The balanced budget identity

// Operating side — balanced by construction operatingRevenue = gifts + grants + events + contributed + AOR releases totalExpenses = scholarshipAwards + operatingExpenses endowmentDraw = max(0, totalExpenses − operatingRevenue) // the residual totalRevenue = operatingRevenue + endowmentDraw operatingNetIncome = totalRevenue − totalExpenses // = 0, always // Balance side marketAppreciation = beginningEndowment × marketReturnPct endingEndowment = beginningEndowment + marketAppreciation − endowmentDraw + additionsToEndowment // Diagnostic rates (emerge from the math, never inputs) policyRate = endowmentDraw / rollingAverageBalance // NACUBO convention actualRate = endowmentDraw / beginningEndowment // financial statement convention

Why residual, not rate-driven

The May 2026 Board-approved budget shows a 10.34% implied policy rate (against the 3-year rolling average) for FY27. That number emerges from the budget identity, not a Finance policy choice. $12.08M total expenses minus $4.66M operating revenue = $7.43M draw; divide by the $71.8M rolling-average balance and you get 10.34%. For context: NACUBO surveys ~700 endowments annually and reports a median spending rate of ~4.7%. DC CAP's FY27 10.34% reflects a trough year in scholarship commitments rather than a structural overdraw — the trajectory below shows the rate bending into the NACUBO yellow band (~5.8%) by FY29 and settling near ~6.4% by FY35.

As scholarships step down (FY27 $5.44M → FY28 $4.5M → FY29 $4.0M) and operating expenses creep ~3% per year, the needed draw bends with them. Under Base assumptions:

YearNeeded drawPolicy rateActual rate
FY27$7,426,79810.34%7.43%
FY28$6,686,1307.68%6.62%

The story this tells: FY27's 10.34% is temporary. The trajectory bends down through FY29 as scholarship commitments wind down, then drifts back up as operating expenses compound. By FY35 the implied rate settles in the ~6.3–6.4% band — yellow on NACUBO bands, manageable.

Why this matters — what the prior model did wrong

An earlier version of the model treated the draw rate as an input (10.34% × rolling avg = draw). That mechanically pulled more from the endowment every year as the rolling average grew — even though scholarship costs were stepping down. The trajectory looked like DC CAP was over-extracting on autopilot. Fixed 2026-05-13: the draw is residual financing, not a rate × balance product.

The Pre-Booked AOR timing trap

$3.87M of Pre-Booked AOR sits on the books from prior years' raise. Of that, only $1.87M releases to FY27 P&L — the rest is multi-year pledge dollars deferred to FY28+. Anyone reading the Pipeline Tool's "$3.87M Pre-Booked" number and inferring "we have $3.87M of FY27 revenue locked in" is wrong. The endowment "gap" of $7.43M is the residual after both new fundraising and pre-booked releases — not after the full $3.87M of pledges.

Pipeline Tool's Summary tab — partially disregarded

The Summary tab's top cross-segment block (R6–R9) is internally inconsistent with the per-segment tabs (Corp $1M Summary vs $650K segment; Individual $400K Summary vs $200K segment). The segment tabs are canonical; the Summary R6–R9 block is disregarded going forward. Tracy and Sashia are reconciling at source.

The Summary tab's year-over-year block (R12–R30) — FY26 / FY27 / FY28 / FY29 AOR totals split by Pre-Booked + New — IS internally consistent. It's usable as a Dev Office aspirational target for FY28+ (e.g., $6.25M FY28, $6.35M FY29) but it's not Finance-approved budget. Finance budgets one year at a time.

"$6.66M raised, $12.08M spent" looks like a $5.4M gap. It's not. §05 · Decisions

What we decided, and why.

Every modeling choice carries a tradeoff. The decisions below are the ones that materially shape what the tool says — recorded so future readers don't have to re-derive them.

D1 · Draw is residual, not rate-driven

Decision: The endowment draw is computed as max(0, totalExpenses − operatingRevenue). The "draw rate" is a diagnostic that emerges, not a knob.

Why: DC CAP sizes the draw to operational need each year. Treating the rate as an input meant the draw mechanically grew with the rolling average — pulling more from the endowment regardless of actual need.

Date: 2026-05-13 (mid-session correction). ADR-020 in BRAIN decisions.md.

D2 · 3-year rolling average (not 5)

Decision: The diagnostic policy rate is computed against a 3-year rolling average of prior ending balances. The 5-year basis (which the Board-approved budget uses) shows up only as a reconciliation comparator.

Why: DC CAP's endowment is young — it grew from ~$60M to ~$100M with the FY26 major gift. A 5-year average suppresses the denominator by ~$32M of pre-major-gift years. 3-year better reflects the new capacity, per NACUBO best practice for young endowments.

Tradeoff: Less smoothing — market drawdowns will move the implied rate faster. Honest and visible.

D3 · GEAR UP excluded

Decision: The model uses the budget's "without GEAR UP" panel. The $1.42M GEAR UP government grant and the matching $1.42M of GEAR UP expenses are pass-through and excluded from this model.

Why: GEAR UP is a federal grant with prescribed pass-through accounting. Including it would inflate apparent revenue and expenses without changing net operating reality.

D4 · Pipeline Tool Summary tab — top block disregarded

Decision: The segment tabs (Foundations, Corporate, Individual) are canonical for FY27 numbers. The Summary tab's top cross-segment block (R6–R9) is disregarded due to internal inconsistencies with the segments.

Why: R6–R9 contains aspirational New AOR totals that don't reconcile to the segment-level details. Tracy and Sashia reconciling at source.

D5 · Composite op-ex (not departmental)

Decision: Operating expenses are modeled as a single composite ($6.64M FY27 baseline, 3% annual growth, one-time $500K cut in FY29 for lease end). Departmental breakdown (Operations / F&D / R&I / Student Success) is not modeled.

Why: Composite is sufficient for multi-year projection. Department-level forecasting would require a per-department headcount/budget model that doesn't exist yet. Future enhancement.

Composition (FY27, for reference): Payroll 52%, Professional Fees 20%, G&A 10%, Rent 8%, IT 5%, Travel 3%, Program 2%.

D6 · Restricted vs unrestricted gifts — abstracted

Decision: The model distinguishes two categories: operating revenue (hits P&L this year) and restricted-to-corpus (goes to endowment principal). Further restriction classes (temporarily restricted, board-designated, etc.) are not modeled.

Why: For board-level projection, the operating-vs-corpus split is the load-bearing distinction. Finer restriction classes affect accounting presentation but not the operational draw math.

D7 · Endowment runway floor (default $40M)

Decision: The Pessimistic scenario on Scenario Overview projects whether the endowment stays above the $40M floor through FY35. This is an internal sanity threshold, not a Board-set policy. A prior Monte Carlo Stress Test that computed P(endowment ≥ floor) probabilistically was retired 2026-05-13 — the deterministic scenario carries the floor-crossing story without the analytical complexity.

Why: $40M is "board-considered policy minimum — confirm in Finance Committee" — pegged to a level below which the Board would face hard operational choices. Editable in the inputs.

D8 · Cloudflare Access gating

Decision: The tool sits behind Cloudflare Access at dccapinnovation.org/financial_modeling/. Allow policy includes @dccap.org and explicit board emails.

Why: The model contains forward-looking projections, endowment balances, and donor-specific fundraising goals. Not appropriate for public access. Cloudflare Access is real auth — not a client-side gate.

Full ADRs in BRAIN decisions.md

The decisions above are the public-facing summary. Full architectural decision records (ADR-020 specifically for the residual-draw correction) live in ~/Desktop/BRAIN/decisions.md and the project-level PLAN.md §11. Refresh the wiki when decisions change.

§06 · Not in scope

What this tool deliberately doesn't do.

Scope discipline matters. The list below is what the tool is not — clarifications to prevent misreading the outputs or asking the model questions it shouldn't answer.

No FERPA / no PII

The tool contains zero student records. The only PII-adjacent data is aggregate fundraising goals and donor categories — never individual scholar information.

No Salesforce integration

Salesforce holds DC CAP's scholar records and detailed donor history. The model never reads from or writes to Salesforce. Pipeline data flows one-way from the Dev Office workbook on SharePoint.

No predictive likelihood scoring

The Dev Office tier categories (Very Likely / Somewhat Likely / Possible / Cultivation) are inclusion gates — Floor includes Tier 1, Base adds Tier 2, Stretch adds Tier 3. No probability-weighted expected values; full dollars at the cutoff.

No multi-year pledge linking

The model treats each year's operating revenue as a number. It does not track which dollars come from which prior-year pledge (the source workbook does that bookkeeping; the model just reads the resulting yearly totals).

No campaign overlay

If DC CAP launches a future named campaign for FY28+ (e.g., a $10M endowment campaign), modeling it requires a separate input flow that doesn't exist yet. The current default holds operating revenue flat at FY27 for simplicity.

No department-level projection

Operating expenses are a composite. The tool doesn't project Operations / Fundraising / Research / Student Success separately. Departmental modeling is a future enhancement.

When NOT to use this tool

For audited financial statements, use the audit packet. For grant-application historicals, use the most recent 990. For scholar-level analysis, use the Scholar Disbursements data extract (FERPA-governed, separate process). This tool is forward-looking projection only.

§07 · Cadence

How this stays current.

Three update rhythms keep the tool in sync with reality: weekly (pipeline), annually (budget), and on-demand (when assumptions move).

1

Weekly · Monday morning · FY27 Pipeline refresh

Preston (or whoever's covering) drags the latest FY27 DC CAP Revenue Goals*.xlsx from the OneDrive SharePoint sync into BRAIN, then runs ./scripts/weekly_pipeline_refresh.sh. The script parses the workbook, runs the verification gate, commits to both repos (no push), and prints the next push commands. Total time ~90 seconds.

2

Annually · May–June · Budget integration

When the Board approves the next fiscal year's budget, update the calibration targets in model.ts DEFAULT_INPUTS: operating revenue subtotal, scholarship awards, operating expense baseline, restricted principal additions, prior-year balances for the rolling average. Run npm test — the FY27 calibration test will fail if values drift > $50K; that's the signal to update.

3

On-demand · When framings shift

If a structural assumption changes — a new ADR, a methodology pivot, a major-gift commitment that requires modeling — log it in PLAN.md, update decisions.md with the rationale, and update this wiki so the next person reading the tool isn't reasoning from stale framing. The wiki is the accessible doc; the PLAN is the engineering doc; decisions.md is the architectural ledger.

4

Continuously · Tests

48 unit tests in src/lib/__tests__/model.test.ts cover FY27 budget calibration, residual-draw math, rolling-average correctness, balance identity, edge cases. Any change to model.ts runs them. If they fail, the change is held back.

How to know if the tool is current

Look at the FY27 Pipeline tab header — it shows "File modified [date] · synced [date]" pulled from the latest parse. If the file-modified date is older than this week and there are known pipeline movements, that's the signal to run the refresh script. If the budget calibration test fails on npm test, that's the signal to absorb a new budget.
