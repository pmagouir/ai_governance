Before you build

Five questions.
Three "yes" or you put it down.

01
Frequency

3+ times per quarter, ongoing? Less than monthly is a one-shot.

02
Variability

Stable shape, varying inputs? Or genuinely different every time?

03
Inheritance

A specific named teammate other than you? Or a private speed-up?

04
Stakes

Could an error reach a stakeholder before a human catches it?

05
Substitution

Better tool already exists? Don't build a Claude version of Salesforce.

The deliberate "no" is leadership, not failure. The wrong build wastes the team's time worse than no build at all.

Read the score

Build · Pause · Pass.

Three verdicts. Each one is a real choice. Calling it a pass is the same kind of leadership move as shipping a build.

3+ yes

Build it.

The candidate earns the investment. Move to the customization plan. Three vectors. Ship v1. Watch a teammate run it.

1-2 yes

Pause. Reframe.

Either narrow the audience until inheritance is a clear yes, or build private and revisit. Don't sell the team a build that hasn't earned the label.

0 yes

Pass. Pick again.

Not the right candidate. Calling pass protects the team's time when you do ship one. Pick a different candidate from the last two weeks.

Build 01 · Project · reuses context

Partner Engagement.

Setup · custom instructions

A workspace loaded with the 13-partner roster, voice rules, verified numbers, and the calendar of who expects what when. Same body of knowledge, many shapes of ask, for months.

What it answers

Drafts. Summaries. Comparisons. Briefings. Four shapes against one workspace.

Steal this

A Project is justified when one workspace handles three or more shapes of ask. If your candidate answers just one shape, it's a Skill.

# Custom instructions (paste into Claude Project) You help DC CAP's Student Success team manage year-round relationships with 13 university partner contacts. Tone: collegial, professional, warm. These are people we will work with for years. Always: name relationship history when relevant; include specific dates and named next steps; check verified DC CAP numbers before quoting impact data. Never: include individual student names, financial aid amounts, or anything beyond Tier 3 unless I load it for a single conversation. # References loaded - partners_2026.md (Tier 3) - dccap_voice.md (Tier 4) - verified_numbers_FY25 (Tier 3) - moil_calendar_2026.md (Tier 3)
Build 02 · Skill · reuses the move

Counselor Cycle Update.

The procedural spine

A SKILL.md anyone can run when DC CAP needs to send a counselor a cycle update — application open, deadline, decision, thank-you. Five cycle moments. Three rules each for "good" and "avoid." One length.

What it produces

A 4-6 sentence email. Names the cycle moment in the opener. One date. One action. Closes warm.

Steal this

Write the avoid list before the good list. The negative space is where your tribal knowledge actually lives — "never use 'unlock,' never plural CTAs, never 'per our records.'"

--- name: counselor-cycle-update-email description: Drafts a DC CAP cycle update email to a high school counselor. --- ## When to trigger Application open · T-2 weeks · T-3 days · decision · partnership thank-you. ## What good looks like - Opens with the relationship. - Names the cycle moment in sentence 1. - One specific date. One specific action. - 4-6 sentences. Always. - Closes warm; doesn't require a meeting. ## What to avoid - Equity framing → use accessibility/impact. - Banned words: unlock, leverage, seamless, journey, elevate, foster, vibrant. - Plural CTAs. - "Per our records" / "I am reaching out to."
Build 03 · Deep Skill · reuses the move with depth

Funder-Framing Drafter.

Depth means every layer has a home

Funder-type register goes in workflow shape. Verified numbers go in org context. Outside research goes in references. The Diligence step ("verify every metric") goes in the procedure body. The "things we learned the hard way" go in avoid.

What the team inherits

A new hire produces a defensible draft on day three. Numbers right. Voice right. Funder register matched. The implicit knowledge that used to live in two heads is in the file.

Steal this

Walk through your build and ask: where does this layer live? If the answer is "in someone's head," that is the layer to encode next.

--- name: funder-framing-paragraph description: Drafts program-impact paragraphs for grant proposals. Detects funder type and matches register. Uses verified numbers only. --- ## Funder-type register Foundation: methodology + theory of change. Corporate: scale + business case. Government: compliance + federal data alignment. Individual: human stakes + named scholar arcs. ## The Diligence step Before the paragraph leaves the Skill: verify every number against verified_numbers_FY25.md. If asked something not in the verified file, say so and route to director — do not estimate. ## References loaded - verified_numbers_FY25.md (Tier 3) - dccap_voice.md (Tier 4) - mathematica_REL_2025_010 (Tier 4) - dccap_program_model_brief (Tier 3) - funder_taxonomy_examples (Tier 4)
Where the floor gets raised

Outside sources
are the customization multiplier.

The difference between a Skill that saves five minutes and one that replaces a whole layer of tribal knowledge is almost always the outside source you loaded.

Research & data

Federal datasets, peer-reviewed papers, evaluation reports.

Mathematica, IES, NCES, the Brookings reports we cite. Loaded as PDFs or summarized into a reference doc. Raises the floor on accuracy and citation discipline.

Peer-org playbooks

How comparable orgs handle the same move.

Two grant narratives, three counselor email exemplars, a partner-engagement playbook from a bigger college access org. Loaded as reference. Anchors the voice and structure.

Authoritative style

Brand guides, voice docs, Anthropic skill-creator standards.

The dccap-brand voice rules. Council on Foundations RFP language standards. Anthropic's own skill-authoring guidance. Loaded as references; cited inside the procedure.

Internal corpus

What we have already approved.

Three years of grant narratives that won. The board memos that landed. The student emails that generated the warmest replies. Outside the build itself, but inside the org. Loaded as exemplars.
